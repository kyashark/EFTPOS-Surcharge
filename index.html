Is Your EFTPOS Surcharge Too High? Here's What the Law Actually Says

Australian businesses are charging billions in payment surcharges — but many are breaking the law. What you need to know as a consumer or business owner.

By Finance Desk  ·  April 2026  ·  8 min read

Next time you tap your card and see a small surcharge pop up on the screen, you might just accept it and move on. But here's what most Australians don't know: a lot of those surcharges are actually illegal.

The Australian Government, backed by the Australian Competition and Consumer Commission (ACCC), has very clear rules about how much a business can charge you when you pay by card — and many businesses are quietly exceeding those limits every single day.

The Basic Rule: You Can Only Charge What It Costs You

Under the Competition and Consumer Act 2010, it is illegal for any Australian business — large or small — to charge a card surcharge that exceeds their actual "cost of acceptance." This is the real cost the business pays to process your payment. Nothing more, nothing less.

Australian Government — ACCC

"Businesses are not allowed to charge more than their actual costs to process a payment."

This rule was first introduced in 2017 and applies to EFTPOS, Mastercard, Visa, and certain American Express cards. The ACCC is the watchdog responsible for enforcing it — and in 2025, cracking down on excessive surcharges became one of their top enforcement priorities.

What's Illegal to Include in a Surcharge?

❌ Businesses CANNOT Charge for These

  • ✕POS Hardware Costs — The cost of your payment terminal, iPad stand, or EFTPOS machine cannot be passed on to customers as a surcharge.
  • ✕Extra Margin or Profit — A surcharge cannot be used as a sneaky way to earn extra revenue. It's strictly cost recovery only.
  • ✕Software & Business Tools — Fees for inventory management systems, data analytics tools, or invoicing software bundled with a payment plan cannot be included.
  • ✕Flat 2.2% or Industry Averages — Simply applying a round number without proving your actual costs is a violation.

✅ What CAN Be Included in Cost of Acceptance

  • ✓Merchant service fees charged by your bank or payment service provider (PSP)
  • ✓Terminal rental and maintenance fees directly tied to card payment processing
  • ✓Card payment fraud prevention service fees
  • ✓Other verifiable direct costs shown on your merchant statement

What Are Typical Legitimate Surcharge Rates?

The Reserve Bank of Australia (RBA) provides guidance on what it typically costs merchants to accept different card types. These aren't hard limits — your actual costs may differ — but they give a useful benchmark:

So if a café is charging you 2.2% on a simple debit card tap — as shown in the viral warning image circulating online — that almost certainly exceeds their actual cost and is likely illegal.

The Consequences for Businesses Breaking the Rules

⚠ Penalties for Excessive Surcharging

The ACCC takes enforcement action against businesses that overcharge, ranging from infringement notices to court-ordered penalties.

The ACCC has already taken action against well-known companies including Fitness First, Nine Entertainment, Europcar, Red Balloon, and Lloyds Auctioneers for excessive surcharging. Small businesses are equally liable — the ACCC made it clear that company size does not exempt anyone from these rules.

The Big Change Coming in 2026

The entire surcharging landscape is about to change dramatically. After a sweeping review of Australia's payment system, the RBA has proposed an outright ban on EFTPOS, Mastercard, and Visa surcharges.

The reforms also include lower interchange fee caps for merchants and greater transparency from payment service providers — addressing long-standing complaints that PSPs have been bundling non-payment services into their fees, inflating the "cost of acceptance" merchants report.

What Should You Do Right Now?

If You're a Consumer:

👀

Watch the Surcharge %

If a debit card surcharge exceeds ~0.5–1%, question it. Anything near 2%+ on debit is almost certainly excessive.

📢

Report It to the ACCC

Lodge a complaint at accc.gov.au. Your report contributes to enforcement action and helps protect other consumers.

💳

Use EFTPOS to Save

Insert your card and select "Savings" instead of tapping — this routes through the cheaper EFTPOS network and may attract a lower surcharge.

💸

Pay Cash if Available

Where accepted, cash remains surcharge-free. Some businesses offer a cash discount precisely because they want to reduce card fees.

If You're a Business Owner:

Check your merchant statement now. Your bank or PSP should provide a statement showing your actual cost of acceptance as a percentage. Use that figure — and only that figure — as your surcharge. If you're charging more, you're legally exposed.

Key obligations every business must meet:

  • Your surcharge must never exceed your actual cost of acceptance
  • You must be able to prove your costs if asked
  • If you charge a single rate across multiple card types, it must be the lowest of all individual rates
  • Surcharges must be clearly disclosed before the customer pays
  • If no surcharge-free payment option exists, include the minimum surcharge in the displayed price
Important Note for Business Owners

With the October 2026 ban approaching, now is the time to start planning how your pricing will absorb payment processing costs directly — whether through slightly adjusted retail prices or by negotiating better rates with your PSP.

The Bottom Line

The message from the Australian Government is unambiguous: surcharging is a cost-recovery tool, not a profit centre. Whether you're a corner café or a national retailer, the rules are the same. You can only charge what it actually costs you to process a payment — and you must be able to prove it.

With the ACCC making surcharge enforcement a top 2025–26 priority, and a full surcharge ban arriving in October 2026, Australian businesses and consumers alike need to understand these rules today.

If something feels wrong at the checkout — it probably is. And now you know exactly what to do about it.
